LO 1Who are a company's stakeholders? Give at least four examples.
+
Stakeholders are all parties who are affected by or have an interest in a company's decisions. Key groups include:
  • Employees — competitive wages, layoff conditions, diversity & inclusion
  • Customers — transparency, no exploitation of limited competition, avoiding addictive products
  • Suppliers — fair treatment throughout the supply chain
  • Local & regional communities — investment in the areas where the firm operates
  • The environment — minimising ecological harm
  • Government — contributing to national finances through taxation
LO 2What are Friedman's three arguments for shareholder capitalism?
+
  • Stakeholder investment is required anyway: Maximising shareholder value already requires investing in customers and employees, since those relationships generate future cash flows.
  • Government fills the gap: Democratic governments use laws and taxes to address externalities — corporations need not duplicate that role.
  • Freedom for investors: Maximising profit gives shareholders maximum personal flexibility to support whichever social objectives they care about.
Note: Friedman did not advocate exploiting stakeholders — he assumed well-functioning markets and governments would prevent abuse.
LO 2What is Enlightened Shareholder Value (ESV), and what are its two practical advantages?
+
ESV holds that investing in stakeholders is how you maximise shareholder value — stakeholders are a means to an end, not an end in themselves. A company invests only when doing so is a positive-NPV project.

Two practical advantages:
  • Clear investment decision criterion: positive NPV
  • Clear performance benchmark: stock price
Example: ESV favours (b) over (a) — paying employees $10m extra raises shareholder value from $100m to $105m — but not (c) over (a) — paying employees $25m extra reduces shareholder value to $95m.
LO 3What are the three violations of Friedman's assumptions that justify stakeholder capitalism?
+
  • Governments may not function well: Corporate lobbying, infrequent elections, and the difficulty of regulating qualitative issues weaken the assumption that government reliably enforces responsible behaviour.
  • Companies do have comparative advantages in serving society: They directly control their operations (e.g., emissions, working conditions) and possess specialist expertise that lets them create social value more efficiently than government.
  • NPV calculation is often impossible: It can be impractical to calculate the NPV impact of stakeholder investments under uncertainty. Treating stakeholders as intrinsically valuable sidesteps this and provides a useful decision heuristic.
LO 3What is the key challenge or criticism of stakeholder capitalism?
+
Without a clear rule to replace NPV, accountability becomes diffuse — "accountability to everyone means accountability to no one." Two forms of arbitrariness arise:
  • Weighting: No agreed formula for how much to weight shareholders vs stakeholders.
  • Comparability: Shareholder value is in USD; stakeholder value (wellbeing, environmental quality) is not directly comparable.
There are also contradictory stakeholder interests — e.g., shutting a coal plant benefits the environment but harms workers and local communities.
LO 4How does Responsible Business differ from pure shareholder capitalism and from CSR?
+
Responsible Business is a hybrid: it creates value for shareholders by creating value for society, while retaining an explicit duty to shareholders.
  • vs Shareholder capitalism: Stakeholders are treated as ends in themselves, not just instruments of profit.
  • vs CSR: CSR covers non-core, peripheral activities (e.g., charitable donations) — it splits the pie. Responsible Business embeds social value creation into the firm's core strategy — it grows the pie.
  • In short: value creation vs value extraction.
LO 4What are the three guiding principles of Responsible Business?
+
  • Multiplication: Does the investment generate a social benefit greater than its private cost? The social return must exceed the private cost.
  • Comparative Advantage: Can the company deliver more value through this activity than others (e.g., government or NGOs)? Ensures social benefits also exceed social costs.
  • Materiality: Are the affected stakeholders material to the business? Makes it more likely that social value created will ultimately convert into shareholder value.
All three together create a virtuous cycle between shareholders and stakeholders.
LO 5What are the three main problems with ESG-linked executive pay?
+
In 2023, 76% of S&P 500 firms linked executive pay to at least one ESG target. The risks:
  • Hitting the target, missing the point: CEOs focus narrowly on the contracted metrics and neglect broader ESG behaviour.
  • Extrinsic not intrinsic motivation: Improvements are pursued for the bonus, not because the CEO genuinely cares.
  • More pay, not more ESG: Information asymmetry allows CEOs to negotiate easy targets — producing higher pay without meaningful progress.
LO 5Why are ESG ratings unreliable? What are the three sources of inconsistency?
+
Correlation between ratings from different agencies is only 0.38–0.71, vs 0.9 for credit ratings (Berg et al., 2022). Sources of divergence:
  • Scope (38%): Different agencies assess different attributes entirely.
  • Measurement (56%): The same dimension is quantified differently — e.g., labour practices measured by turnover vs labour violations.
  • Weighting (6%): Different providers assign different weights to the components.
LO 5What formal structures can companies adopt to commit to Responsible Business?
+
  • Benefit Corporations: A legal structure requiring directors to deliver on stated societal benefits beyond profit maximisation.
  • B Corps: No legal status, but certified by B Lab (a global nonprofit) based on verified social and environmental performance.
  • Purpose Statements: Companies can publish a public purpose statement without formal legal or certification requirements.
Even without these, companies can voluntarily report stakeholder value using frameworks such as IFRS Sustainability Standards — though comparability across firms remains a significant ongoing challenge.

Short answer · 4 marks
Explain the difference between Responsible Business and Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR). In your answer, refer to the concept of "growing vs splitting the pie."
CSR involves non-core, peripheral activities such as charitable donations — it takes existing profits and redistributes them, thereby splitting the pie between shareholders and society without increasing total value.

Responsible Business, by contrast, embeds social value creation into the firm's core strategy. By treating stakeholders well as part of its primary business activity, the firm generates more total value — it grows the pie — so both shareholders and stakeholders can be better off simultaneously.

Responsible Business retains an explicit duty to shareholders but argues that social value creation is the mechanism through which shareholder value is generated in the long run, rather than a diversion from it.
Short answer · 6 marks
A pharmaceutical company wishes to invest in free vaccines for low-income communities. Using the three principles of Responsible Business, assess whether this investment is likely to be justified.
Multiplication: The social benefit (reduced disease burden, improved public health outcomes) likely far exceeds the firm's private cost of producing the vaccines — the benefit multiplies beyond what the company spends. ✓

Comparative Advantage: A pharmaceutical firm has unique R&D expertise and production capacity in vaccine development. It can deliver this social benefit more effectively and efficiently than a government agency or NGO. ✓

Materiality: For a pharmaceutical company, public health is directly material to its core business — reputational gains, regulatory goodwill, talent attraction, and future market development in emerging economies all convert the social value into long-run shareholder value. ✓

All three principles are satisfied. Responsible Business would support the investment.
Short answer · 2 marks
Name two parties who protect stakeholder welfare under shareholder capitalism, and briefly explain how each does so.
1. The elected government — enacts laws and taxes (e.g., minimum wage, environmental regulation) through democratic processes to ensure socially responsible corporate behaviour.

2. Shareholders themselves — when profits and wealth are maximised, shareholders have maximum personal flexibility to allocate their wealth to the social causes they care about (charitable giving, ethical investment, etc.).
Conceptual · 3 marks
The stakeholder capitalism pie diagram shows three scenarios: (a) shareholders $60m / stakeholders $40m, (b) shareholders $50m / stakeholders $50m, (c) shareholders $70m / stakeholders $70m. Which scenario best illustrates the long-run case for stakeholder capitalism, and why?
Scenario (c) best illustrates the long-run case. Starting from (a), the firm reduces shareholder returns to invest more in stakeholders — moving to (b), where the pie is split equally but the total is unchanged. However, in the long run, this investment grows the total pie: in (c) both shareholders and stakeholders receive $70m, more than either group received in (a).

This demonstrates that intrinsic investment in stakeholders — even when short-run NPV cannot be calculated — can create total value that benefits all parties. ESV would have rejected the move from (a) to (b) since shareholder value falls in the short term; stakeholder capitalism, by taking the long view, ultimately produces a better outcome for everyone.
